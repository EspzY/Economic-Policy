## Overview

Lecture 7 combines two advanced but connected ideas:

1. forward guidance as an expectations-management tool at or near the lower bound
2. the empirical and conceptual challenge of identifying the slope of the Phillips curve

Both topics are about expectations, identification, and policy credibility, not just formula memorization.

## Part A: Forward guidance in the lower-bound environment

### A1. Why unconventional communication matters

The forward-guidance slides start from a low-rate world where large negative demand shocks can push policy into a
liquidity-trap-like region. In that environment, future policy promises can affect current outcomes through expectations.

Canonical block shown in the lecture:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa x_t + u_t^{\pi}
$$

$$
x_t = E_t x_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1} - r_t^n)
$$

with a non-negativity/lower-bound constraint on policy rates.

### A2. Delphic versus Odyssean forward guidance

The slides define:

- Delphic guidance: central-bank forecast communication, no commitment
- Odyssean guidance: commitment-type communication meant to shape future policy expectations

Policy language in exams:

- Delphic = information channel
- Odyssean = commitment channel

Time-contingent and state-contingent forms are both discussed in the lecture.

### A3. Forward-guidance puzzle (complementary lecture)

The complementary note rewrites the simplified model and solves forward:

$$
y_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\sum_{s=t}^{\infty} E_t r_s
$$

$$
\pi_t = \kappa \sum_{s=t}^{\infty}\beta^{s-t}E_t y_s
$$

This implies very strong effects from news about distant future rates. A far-horizon rate cut can have very large
current effects in the baseline model. That is the forward-guidance puzzle: theoretical power that looks unrealistically
large in many practical settings.

### A4. Direction of current research in the note

The complementary lecture shows discounting-style extensions:

$$
\pi_t = \alpha_{\pi}\beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa y_t
$$

$$
y_t = \alpha_y E_t y_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1})
$$

with $0<\alpha_{\pi},\alpha_y<1$, which attenuates distant-horizon effects.

Economic mechanisms discussed:

- finite horizons / mortality risk
- cognitive inattention
- credit constraints / incomplete markets

## Part B: The slope of the Phillips curve

### B1. Structural equation versus reduced-form correlation

The lecture states the modern NKPC form:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa x_t + u_t^{\pi}
$$

or in unemployment-gap language:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} - \kappa_u \tilde{u}_t + u_t^{\pi}
$$

The parameter of interest is the structural slope ($\kappa$ or $\kappa_u$), not raw scatterplot correlation.

### B2. Identification challenges highlighted in the slides

The lecture emphasizes three major omitted-variable/identification issues:

1. expected inflation is hard to observe
2. natural output or supply-side drivers are hard to observe
3. cost-push shocks are often unobserved

These can bias naive slope estimates toward zero or even misleading signs.

### B3. Why “flattening” claims require care

Episodes like “missing inflation” or “missing disinflation” can reflect:

- expectation shifts
- policy-regime changes
- supply disturbances

not only changes in structural slope.

## Exam-level interpretation template

When asked whether the Phillips curve flattened:

1. distinguish reduced-form correlation from structural slope
2. state identification problems explicitly
3. explain at least one channel that can shift correlation without changing slope
4. only then discuss whether slope change is still plausible

This template matches the style of the Lecture 7 discussion and the corresponding exam-style question framing.

## Common pitfalls

- Calling all guidance “commitment” (confusing Delphic and Odyssean).
- Ignoring the lower-bound context when motivating forward guidance.
- Treating forward-guidance puzzle algebra as literal policy magnitude without model-friction caveats.
- Reading scatterplots as direct estimates of structural $\kappa$.
