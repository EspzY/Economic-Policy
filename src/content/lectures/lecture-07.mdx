## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 7 combines two topics that look different but share the same deep skill: **reasoning about expectations and identification**.

1. **Forward guidance**: how policy communication about *future* interest rates affects inflation and output *today*, especially when the policy rate is constrained by a lower bound.
2. **Phillips-curve slope**: how to interpret inflation and unemployment/output-gap evidence without confusing a reduced-form correlation with the structural slope parameter.

If you learn this lecture well, you become much harder to trick on the exam. Many exam questions are essentially:

- "Explain a policy argument that relies on expectations" (forward guidance, credibility).
- "Explain what evidence does and does not identify" (Phillips curve flattening, anchored expectations, supply shocks).

## Part A: Forward Guidance (What It Is And Why It Works)

### A1. Definition (slides)

The forward-guidance slides define forward guidance as communication about the **future path of monetary policy instruments**.
The key model sentence is: expectations about future policy matter massively for outcomes today (Woodford-style logic in the slides).

### A2. Delphic vs Odyssean (slides)

The slides make a distinction you should be able to say in one sentence on the exam:

- **Delphic forward guidance**: forecast-style communication about likely outcomes; **no commitment**.
- **Odyssean forward guidance**: commitment-oriented communication intended to shape future policy expectations.

The slides also distinguish:

- **time-contingent**: "low until date $T$"
- **state-contingent**: "low until condition $C$ holds" (for example an unemployment threshold)

### A3. Why the lower bound makes communication powerful (slides)

In the slides’ "in our model" block, forward guidance is analyzed using the canonical NK structure *with a lower bound on the nominal rate*.
One slide writes the system as (perfect-foresight version shown in the deck):

$$
\pi_t = \beta \pi_{t+1} + \kappa x_t + u_t^{\pi}
$$

$$
x_t = x_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(i_t - \pi_{t+1} - r_t^n)
$$

and the lower-bound constraint (in the lecture’s max form):

$$
i_t = \max\{r_t^n + \phi_\pi \pi_t + \phi_y x_t,\; 0\}.
$$

Economic meaning:

- if the natural real rate $r_t^n$ becomes very negative (large demand shock), the desired policy rate may be negative, but the lower bound prevents it
- then the economy can sit with a negative output gap and inflation below target
- **forward guidance** works by shifting expectations of future inflation and future policy, which shifts both the IS relation (via the real rate) and the Phillips curve (via expected inflation)

This is also why the slides highlight time inconsistency: promising "lower for longer" is useful, but credibility is required.

## Step-By-Step Derivation: The Forward-Guidance Puzzle (Complementary Lecture)

The complementary "forward guidance puzzle" note takes the NK structure and strips away extra terms to isolate the expectations channel.
It sets aside technology, discount-factor shocks, and cost-push shocks and focuses on anticipated policy news.

In the simplified system in the note:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa y_t
$$

$$
y_t = E_t y_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1})
$$

Define the real rate $r_t \equiv i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$. Then the IS relation becomes:

$$
y_t = E_t y_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}r_t.
$$

### Step 1 (Solve the IS relation forward: no discounting)

Write the $t+1$ version and take $E_t[\cdot]$:

$$
E_t y_{t+1} = E_t y_{t+2} - \frac{1}{\sigma}E_t r_{t+1}.
$$

Substitute into the time-$t$ equation and iterate. After $T$ substitutions:

$$
y_t = E_t y_{t+T+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}\sum_{k=0}^{T}E_t r_{t+k}.
$$

Impose the standard transversality condition $\lim_{T\to\infty}E_t y_{t+T+1}=0$ to obtain the note’s key equation:

$$
y_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\sum_{s=t}^{\infty}E_t r_s.
$$

Math move: recursive substitution turns a forward-looking difference equation into an infinite sum.

Economic meaning: **current output depends on the entire expected path of future real rates**.
In this baseline form, there is **no discounting** of distant future real rates in the $y_t$ expression.
That is the first ingredient of the puzzle.

### Step 2 (Solve the NKPC forward)

Apply the same recursive-substitution method to:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa y_t.
$$

Iterating forward and imposing $\lim_{T\to\infty}\beta^{T+1}E_t\pi_{t+T+1}=0$ gives:

$$
\pi_t = \kappa \sum_{s=t}^{\infty}\beta^{s-t}E_t y_s.
$$

Economic meaning: inflation today depends on a discounted sum of expected future real activity.
Since $\beta$ is close to 1 in the slides, the discounting is weak, so far-future activity still matters a lot.

### Step 3 (Why distant-horizon forward guidance can be unrealistically powerful)

The note’s punchline is not "forward guidance works." It is: **in this baseline algebra, it works too much**.
Here is a clean way to see that with one shock experiment.

Suppose at time $t$ the central bank credibly announces a one-time reduction in the real rate at horizon $T$:

$$
E_t r_{t+T} \downarrow
$$

and assume the announced path holds in expectations (the thought experiment in the note).

From the solved IS relation,
for each $j=0,1,\dots,T$, the term $r_{t+T}$ appears in the sum for $y_{t+j}$.
So (holding other rates fixed) the change implies:

$$
E_t y_{t+j} \uparrow \quad \text{for many consecutive future periods}.
$$

Now plug into the solved NKPC. The effect on current inflation is proportional to:

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{T}\beta^j,
$$

which grows with the horizon $T$ and is approximately $1/(1-\beta)$ for large $T$.
This is exactly why the note writes a combined expression where long-horizon rates get very large cumulative weights.

Economic meaning: the far-horizon rate cut affects *many* future output terms, and inflation today depends on (almost) the whole path of those outputs.
So the further out the announcement is, the more periods it influences, making the impact grow rather than fade.

## How the note mitigates the puzzle: discounting wedges (Complementary Lecture)

The note then shows one class of "discounting" modifications:

$$
\pi_t = \alpha_{\pi}\beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa y_t
$$

$$
y_t = \alpha_y E_t y_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}),
\qquad 0<\alpha_{\pi},\alpha_y<1.
$$

If you solve the modified IS relation forward, the weights become geometric:

$$
y_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\alpha_y^k E_t r_{t+k}.
$$

So distant-horizon real rates matter less when $\alpha_y<1$.
The note discusses mechanisms that can justify this kind of attenuation (finite horizons, inattention, credit constraints/incomplete markets).

## Part B: The Phillips Curve Slope (Identification, Not Scatterplots)

### B1. Modern NKPC and what the slope means (slides)

The Lecture 7 slides present the modern NKPC as:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa x_t + u_t^{\pi}.
$$

The slide text defines $x_t$ as output relative to efficient output and highlights that **$\kappa$ is the slope**: how much inflation responds causally to real pressure.

The slides also show an unemployment-gap representation (with Okun’s-law reasoning):

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} - \kappa_u \tilde{u}_t + u_t^{\pi}.
$$

### B2. How nominal rigidity links to a smaller slope (slides)

The deck connects the slope to Calvo stickiness:

$$
\kappa = \lambda(\sigma+\phi)
= \frac{(1-\beta\theta)(1-\theta)}{\theta}(\sigma+\phi).
$$

Economic meaning: a higher $\theta$ means prices are stickier on average, which reduces the slope $\kappa$.
Lower $\kappa$ means real activity moves more for a given demand disturbance: the economy is "more Keynesian" in the slide’s wording.

### B3. Identification: why the scatterplot does not reveal κ (slides)

The lecture’s identification warning is very explicit. One slide lists three omitted-variable problems:

1. expected inflation is hard to observe
2. natural output / supply shocks are hard to observe
3. cost-push shocks are often unobserved

All three can create omitted-variable bias in naive slope estimates.

One useful exam-level way to say this: the NKPC is a structural equation with multiple drivers.
If you regress inflation on unemployment (or an output-gap proxy) without controlling for the other drivers, you are not identifying $\kappa$.

### B4. "Did it flatten?" The slides’ four hypotheses

The deck frames flattening as a hypothesis test with four candidate explanations:

1. the structural slope $\kappa$ decreased (the Phillips curve changed)
2. monetary policy was conducted optimally (Phillips curve alive, but stabilized outcomes)
3. cost-push/supply shocks became more important (Phillips curve alive, but correlation shifts)
4. labor market slack is mis-measured (Phillips curve alive, but the x-axis is wrong)

Exam-safe move: you do not need to pick one. You need to show you understand that the observed correlation can change for multiple reasons, many of which do not imply a change in $\kappa$.

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Treating Delphic and Odyssean guidance as the same thing (forecast channel vs commitment channel).
- Using the forward-guidance puzzle algebra as a literal quantitative forecast without stating model-friction caveats.
- Saying "Phillips curve is flat" based on a scatterplot without discussing expected inflation and supply shocks.
- Confusing the structural slope $\kappa$ with a reduced-form correlation between inflation and unemployment.
