## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 10 extends Lecture 9's debt arithmetic into two real-world complications that are extremely exam-relevant:

1. **Incentives (political economy):** debt is not just a financing tool; it can be used strategically to constrain future policy choices.
2. **Risk (default and crises):** the interest rate on debt is not fixed; it can jump when markets reassess default probability, and that jump can itself raise default risk.

If you learn this lecture well, you should be able to do three high-yield exam tasks:

- derive the stable debt-to-GDP condition $(G-T)/Y = (g-r)\\lambda$ step-by-step and interpret the $g-r$ logic carefully
- explain "strategic debt accumulation" in one sentence (what is being constrained and why)
- explain the self-fulfilling crisis loop: higher perceived default risk -> higher required returns -> higher repayment burden -> higher default risk

### Connection to the rest of the course

- Lecture 9 gave you the PV budget constraint and no-Ponzi logic.
- Lecture 10 shows how debt dynamics can change when (i) governments have political incentives and (ii) markets price default risk.
- Later lectures reuse "feedback loop" reasoning (expectations and self-fulfilling dynamics) in different contexts.

## Core Theory: Strategic Debt Accumulation (Political Economy)

The lecture defines political economy as "economic tools applied to politics" and highlights that political processes
can generate inefficient outcomes.

The key mechanism named in the slides is **strategic debt accumulation**:

- a government can finance spending with debt to limit the choices of later governments
- debt becomes a commitment device that reallocates future fiscal space, not just a financing method

Exam-safe wording: strategic debt is about *constraining future policy*, not about "forgetting to balance the budget".

## Step-By-Step Derivation: Sustainable Debt Ratios (Constant λ)

This section answers an exam-style question: **when can a government run a persistent primary deficit and still keep the debt-to-GDP ratio stable?**

### Step 0 (Define the debt ratio)

Nominal GDP is $Y_t^N = P_t Y_t$. Define the nominal debt ratio:

$$
\lambda \equiv \frac{B_t}{Y_t^N}.
$$

### Step 1 (Nominal debt flow equation)

The lecture uses:

$$
\dot B_t = P_t(G_t - T_t) + i_t B_t,
$$

where $P_t(G_t-T_t)$ is the **nominal primary deficit** and $i_t B_t$ is nominal interest servicing.

### Step 2 (Constant ratio implies equal growth rates)

If $\\lambda$ is constant over time, then debt and nominal GDP must grow at the same proportional rate:

$$
\frac{\dot B_t}{B_t} = \frac{\dot Y_t^N}{Y_t^N}.
$$

Nominal GDP growth decomposes into inflation plus real growth:

$$
\frac{\dot Y_t^N}{Y_t^N} = \pi_t + g_t.
$$

So under a constant ratio:

$$
\frac{\dot B_t}{B_t} = \pi_t + g_t
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
\dot B_t = (\pi_t + g_t)B_t.
$$

### Step 3 (Plug into the flow equation and isolate the primary balance)

Insert $\dot B_t=(\\pi_t+g_t)B_t$ into the debt-flow equation:

$$
(\pi_t + g_t)B_t = P_t(G_t - T_t) + i_t B_t.
$$

Rearrange:

$$
P_t(G_t - T_t) = (\pi_t + g_t - i_t)B_t.
$$

Divide by $Y_t^N=P_tY_t$:

$$
\frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} = (\pi_t + g_t - i_t)\lambda.
$$

### Step 4 (Use Fisher to get the slide's (g - r) rule)

Using the Fisher approximation $i_t \\approx r_t + \\pi_t$ gives:

$$
\frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} = (g_t - r_t)\lambda.
$$

Economic meaning (what to say in words):

- if $g>r$, growth can offset the interest burden at a given debt ratio, so a primary deficit can be consistent with a stable ratio
- if $r>g$, keeping the ratio stable requires primary surpluses

The lecture also warns you not to use $g-r$ mechanically: fiscal adjustments can affect $g$, and high debt/default risk can raise $r$ (risk premia).

## Step-By-Step Model: Debt Crises with Default Risk (Multiple Equilibria)

This section builds a default-risk pricing loop that can generate multiple equilibria.

Important notation warning: in this block the slides use $\pi$ for **default probability** (not inflation). Here we
write $\pi^{def}$.

### D1. Investor pricing condition

Let:

- $R$ be the gross interest factor on government debt ($R=1+r$)
- $R'$ be the risk-free gross interest factor
- $\pi^{def}$ be probability of default (assume zero payoff in default in the slide's simplest logic)

The slide's investor condition is:

$$
(1-\pi^{def})R = R'
$$

So higher default probability requires a higher promised return $R$.

### D2. Default probability as a function of required repayment

Let $T$ be uncertain tax income. Default occurs when taxes are insufficient to repay:

$$
T < R D
$$

So:

$$
\pi^{def} = \Pr(T < RD) = F(RD)
$$

where $F(\cdot)$ is the CDF of $T$.

### D3. Equilibrium and multiple equilibria

Equilibrium requires both:

- pricing: $(1-\pi^{def})R=R'$
- repayment risk: $\pi^{def}=F(RD)$

Graphically, this can produce:

- a low-risk equilibrium (stable)
- a higher-risk equilibrium (unstable)
- a collapse equilibrium where markets do not buy debt (the slide describes $R=\infty$, $\pi^{def}=1$)

### D4. Slide implications you should be able to explain

The deck lists four exam-relevant implications:

1. multiple equilibria and self-fulfilling beliefs: higher $\pi^{def}$ implies higher $R$, and higher $R$ increases $\pi^{def}$
2. small differences in fundamentals can generate large outcome differences; some parameter regions can have no interior equilibrium
3. defaults can be "unexpected": intermediate default probabilities can be hard to sustain when uncertainty is low
4. fundamentals still matter: higher $D$, higher $R'$, and weaker fiscal capacity (the distribution $F$) raise default likelihood; the slide argues for prudent debt ratios

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Mixing up inflation $\pi$ with default probability $\pi^{def}$.
- Treating sustainability as a one-period balanced-budget requirement.
- Using $r-g$ arguments without mentioning that $r$ and $g$ can react to fiscal policy and debt levels.
- Describing debt crises as purely "fundamental" or purely "self-fulfilling" (the lecture is explicit that both matter).
