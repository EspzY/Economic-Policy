## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 3 gave you a clean baseline benchmark: **divine coincidence**. In that baseline, stabilizing inflation also stabilizes the output gap.
Lecture 4 is where that clean story breaks in a very controlled way.

The lecture introduces a **cost-push wedge** in the Phillips curve, which creates a real stabilization trade-off: you generally cannot set inflation and the welfare-relevant output gap to zero at the same time.
This trade-off is the core reason later lectures distinguish **discretion vs commitment**, and why "optimal policy" cannot be summarized as "always set inflation to zero."

### Connection to later lectures

- Lecture 5 and beyond revisit the same trade-off under commitment and history dependence.
- Lecture 6 uses the same logic to discuss credibility and time inconsistency.
- In exam problems, this lecture is often where you show you can derive and interpret a targeting rule rather than only quote it.

## Symbols, Parameters, And Gaps (Do Not Mix These Up)

Lecture 4 uses two different "gap" concepts. Getting them wrong is the easiest way to lose points.

| Object | Meaning in Lecture 4 | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| $y_t$ | actual output (sticky prices) | the realized allocation |
| $y_t^n$ | natural output (flexible prices, still monopolistic competition) | the benchmark used in earlier NK output-gap notation |
| $y_t^e$ | efficient output (first-best benchmark) | the welfare benchmark used in the loss function |
| $x_t \equiv y_t - y_t^e$ | welfare-relevant output gap (relative to efficient output) | this is the $x_t$ that enters the objective |
| $\tilde{y}_t \equiv y_t - y_t^n$ | NK output gap (relative to natural output) | useful for NKPC/DIS in earlier lectures |
| $u_t^{\pi}$ | cost-push wedge in NKPC | breaks divine coincidence (creates trade-off) |
| $\alpha_x$ | weight on $x_t^2$ in the loss function | controls how much output stabilization matters relative to inflation |
| $\kappa$ | NKPC slope | governs how much inflation responds to real pressure |
| $\beta$ | discount factor | weights the future in the objective and NKPC |
| $\rho_{\pi}$ | persistence parameter for $u_t^{\pi}$ | affects how painful shocks are over time |

One identity the lecture uses repeatedly is:

$$
\tilde{y}_t = y_t - y_t^n = x_t + (y_t^e - y_t^n).
$$

Economic meaning: the standard NK gap $\tilde{y}_t$ equals the welfare gap $x_t$ plus a wedge between efficient and natural output.
That wedge is exactly what becomes the cost-push term.

## Core Theory: Cost-Push Shocks Create A Stabilization Trade-Off

Lecture 4 rewrites the NKPC in terms of the welfare gap and a cost-push shock:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa x_t + u_t^{\pi}.
$$

The lecture defines the wedge as:

$$
u_t^{\pi} = \kappa (y_t^e - y_t^n),
\qquad
u_t^{\pi} = \rho_{\pi}u_{t-1}^{\pi} + e_t^{\pi}.
$$

Economic meaning: $u_t^{\pi}$ collects forces that push inflation away from what "real pressure" $x_t$ alone would imply
(for example: markup/competition shifts, bargaining shifts, oil/tax wedges, and other supply-side distortions in the slides' interpretation).

Why this breaks divine coincidence (intuition): when $u_t^{\pi} \ne 0$, the Phillips curve has a wedge term that policy cannot remove instantly.
So stabilizing inflation perfectly generally requires accepting a non-zero welfare gap (and vice versa).

## Objective Used In The Slides

The slides evaluate policy using a quadratic loss function:

$$
\min \frac{1}{2}E_t\sum_{i=0}^{\infty}\beta^i\left(\pi_{t+i}^2 + \alpha_x x_{t+i}^2\right).
$$

Interpretation: the policymaker dislikes inflation variability and dislikes welfare-gap variability.
The parameter $\alpha_x$ is the knob that determines how costly it is to move $x_t$ in order to stabilize inflation.

## Step-By-Step Derivation: Discretionary Targeting Rule

Under **discretion**, the central bank re-optimizes period by period and takes expectations as given.
In particular, the lecture packages the forward-looking pieces into:

$$
\pi_t = \kappa x_t + f_t, \quad f_t \equiv \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + u_t^{\pi}.
$$

Math move: rewrite the NKPC so the only choice variable entering the constraint directly is $x_t$.

Economic meaning: under discretion, the central bank treats the private sector's inflation expectations term as given in the one-period problem.
The policy trade-off is then "choose $x_t$ to trade off inflation today against output-gap costs," given the wedge.

Form the one-period Lagrangian:

$$
\mathcal{L}_t = \frac{1}{2}\left(\pi_t^2 + \alpha_x x_t^2\right) + \lambda_t(\pi_t - \kappa x_t - f_t).
$$

FOCs:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_t}{\partial \pi_t} = \pi_t + \lambda_t = 0
$$

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_t}{\partial x_t} = \alpha_x x_t - \kappa \lambda_t = 0.
$$

Substitute $\lambda_t=-\pi_t$ into the second FOC:

$$
\alpha_x x_t + \kappa \pi_t = 0
\Rightarrow
x_t = -\frac{\kappa}{\alpha_x}\pi_t.
$$

Math move: eliminate the multiplier to get a direct relationship between target variables.

Economic meaning: under discretion, the optimal policy choice makes inflation and the welfare gap move with opposite signs.
The slope $\kappa/\alpha_x$ tells you how steep that trade-off is in the optimal targeting rule.

## Optimal Responses Under Cost-Push Shocks (Slides)

The lecture provides the analytical solution under a cost-push shock process:

$$
\pi_t = \frac{\alpha_x}{\kappa^2+\alpha_x(1-\beta\rho_{\pi})}u_t^{\pi}
= \alpha_x \Psi u_t^{\pi}
$$

$$
x_t = -\kappa \Psi u_t^{\pi},
\quad
\Psi = \frac{1}{\kappa^2+\alpha_x(1-\beta\rho_{\pi})}.
$$

Interpretation (sign logic): for a positive cost-push shock $u_t^{\pi}>0$ with positive parameters, inflation rises and the welfare gap falls.
That is the trade-off that was absent under divine coincidence.

## Implementation Through The IS Side (What The Slides Emphasize)

The lecture and supplementary note provide the implied equilibrium interest-rate expression:

$$
i_t = r_t^e + \Psi\left[\sigma\kappa(1-\rho_{\pi})+\alpha_x\rho_{\pi}\right]u_t^{\pi}.
$$

Exam-safe interpretation: even when you have an optimal allocation in terms of $(\pi_t,x_t)$, you still need an implementable instrument rule.
This is why Lecture 4 re-emphasizes uniqueness/determinacy requirements (Lecture 3) and why later lectures focus on commitment rules.

## When Is Strict Inflation Targeting Optimal?

The slides state strict inflation targeting is optimal only in special cases:

1. no cost-push shocks
2. no concern for output-gap variability ($\alpha_x=0$)

Outside those cases, flexible inflation targeting is the relevant logic: you accept some inflation movement to avoid excessively large welfare-gap movements.

## Output Gap In Policy Practice (Complementary Slides)

The complementary output-gap lecture emphasizes that model-based and statistical output gaps are different:

- model gap: $y_t-y_t^n$
- statistical gap: $y_t-y_t^*$ where $y_t^*$ is a trend output estimate

This matters for policy interpretation and for exam answers: "the output gap" is not one unique empirical series.
Measurement noise can contaminate real-time judgments, which is one reason actual policy is cautious.

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Mixing up $x_t$ (welfare gap relative to efficient output) with $\tilde{y}_t$ (gap relative to natural output).
- Forgetting what discretion means here: expectations are taken as given in the period problem.
- Claiming "optimal policy means zero inflation always" even when cost-push shocks are active.
- Treating estimated statistical output gaps as if they were the model gap.

## Mini Worked Reasoning (Exam-Style)

Question: a positive cost-push shock hits. What signs do inflation and $x_t$ take under discretion?

Using:

$$
\pi_t = \alpha_x \Psi u_t^{\pi}, \quad x_t=-\kappa\Psi u_t^{\pi}
$$

with positive parameters, $u_t^{\pi}>0$ implies $\pi_t>0$ and $x_t<0$.

That is exactly the short-run policy tension created by the wedge term in the NKPC.
