## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

The rest of the course is built around a single idea: monetary policy is a stabilization tool that works largely through **expectations**.
Before we can solve models (Lecture 2) or discuss optimal policy (Lectures 4 to 6), we need a clean vocabulary for:

- what the central bank is trying to stabilize (objectives)
- what it can actually set today (instrument)
- how that instrument is moved in a predictable way (rules)
- what part of policy is predictable versus surprising (systematic vs unsystematic)

The slides literally frame the first part of the course as stabilization policy with two guiding questions: **what to stabilize** and **how**.
Lecture 1 is where you learn to answer those questions in precise, exam-grade language.

### Connection to later lectures

- Lecture 2 introduces the 3-equation New Keynesian model where expectations enter directly through the NKPC and DIS equations.
- Lecture 3 shows why rules must anchor expectations (determinacy) and why "optimal allocations" are not automatically implementable rules.
- Lectures 4 to 6 use the same vocabulary to discuss commitment, discretion, credibility, and forward guidance.

## Objectives: What Outcomes Are We Trying To Stabilize?

Lecture 1 starts from real-world mandates and then translates them into the language used in the models later.

### United States (slides)

The slides summarize the Fed's dual mandate as:

- **Stable prices:** 2% per year (PCE inflation in the slide).
- **Maximum employment:** no single fixed number (employment is largely determined by non-monetary factors), so the Fed uses many indicators.

Important bridge to the models:

- the slides mention the unemployment rate (often relative to a "natural rate")
- and the output gap (output relative to potential output)

These are the objects that will later become model-defined "gaps" (for example the output gap).

### Norway (slides)

The slide deck also quotes the Norges Bank mandate and emphasizes **flexible inflation targeting**:

- operational target: annual consumer price inflation close to 2% over time
- forward-looking and flexible: can contribute to high and stable output and employment
- also mentions counteracting the build-up of financial imbalances

Exam-useful language: "inflation targeting can be flexible" means the central bank can care about real activity and financial stability *while still using inflation as the nominal anchor*.

## Instruments: What Can The Central Bank Move Directly?

Lecture 1 keeps the instrument discussion concrete:

- In the US, the conventional instrument is the **Federal Funds Rate target** (an overnight interbank rate in the slides).
- Decisions on the FFR are accompanied by changes in the interest rate paid on reserves held at the Fed (slides).
- The inflation target acts as a **nominal anchor** (the slides mention historical anchors: gold, exchange rates, money growth targets).

The slides also highlight that conventional rate policy hits a constraint at the zero lower bound (ZLB). That is why the lecture previews "new instruments":

- forward guidance
- quantitative easing
- negative interest rates

You do not need to master those tools yet. For Lecture 1, the key is: instruments are about what the central bank can directly set, and the rest of the economy responds through transmission and expectations.

## Symbols, Parameters, And Objects Used In Lecture 1

Lecture 1 uses a simple rule-based notation that is intentionally reusable later. Keep these objects straight:

| Symbol | Meaning (what it is) | Exam-safe way to describe it |
|---|---|---|
| $R_t$ | Policy (nominal) interest rate in the Taylor rule | "The instrument: the central bank's policy rate." |
| $r_t^{LR}$ or $r^*$ | Long-run real natural rate used in the Taylor rule slides | "A long-run real-rate benchmark; the slides treat it as constant in the basic Taylor rule." |
| $\pi_t$ | Inflation | "Inflation (in the slides: change in the log price level)." |
| $\pi^*$ | Inflation target | "The nominal anchor: the desired inflation rate (slides: 2%)." |
| $y_t$ | Output | "Economic activity (often proxied by GDP in applications)." |
| $y_t^p$ | Potential output | "A potential/flexible benchmark used to define an output-gap term." |
| $y_t - y_t^p$ | Output gap term used in the Taylor rule | "Real activity relative to a benchmark (do not confuse with a statistical trend unless the context is empirical)." |
| $\varepsilon_t^M$ | Monetary policy shock | "The unsystematic/surprise component of policy not implied by the usual rule." |

## Step-By-Step Derivation: Rewriting The Taylor Rule Into "Level + Gaps"

The slide deck presents the Taylor rule and then rewrites it to make its logic easier to interpret.
This is simple algebra, but it is high-yield for the exam because it teaches you to separate:

- the neutral/steady-state level of the nominal rate
- the systematic responses to inflation and activity gaps

### Step 1 (Original Taylor form in the slides)

$$
R_t = r_t^{LR} + \pi_t + 0.5(\pi_t - \pi^*) + 0.5(y_t - y_t^p)
$$

Math move: write the rule in "inflation level + gap adjustments" form.

Economic meaning: the policy rate is higher when inflation is higher and when activity is above its benchmark.

### Step 2 (Add and subtract the inflation target)

The slides insert $\pi_t - \pi^* + \pi^*$:

$$
R_t = r_t^{LR} + (\pi_t - \pi^*) + \pi^* + 0.5(\pi_t - \pi^*) + 0.5(y_t - y_t^p)
$$

Math move: adding and subtracting $\pi^*$ does not change the expression; it just reorganizes it.

Economic meaning: this makes the inflation target explicit as the "anchor" term.

### Step 3 (Collect the inflation-gap terms)

$$
R_t = r_t^{LR} + \pi^* + 1.5(\pi_t - \pi^*) + 0.5(y_t - y_t^p)
$$

Math move: $(\pi_t - \pi^*) + 0.5(\pi_t - \pi^*) = 1.5(\pi_t - \pi^*)$.

Economic meaning: the rule says: start from a baseline nominal level $r_t^{LR} + \pi^*$ and then react aggressively to the inflation gap.
That aggressiveness is connected to the Taylor principle.

### The Taylor principle (as stated in the slides)

Lecture 1 highlights this rule-of-thumb principle:

- the policy rate should increase **more than one-for-one** with a persistent increase in inflation

Reason in one sentence (exam-safe): if inflation rises persistently by 1 percentage point and the nominal rate rises by more than 1 point, the **real** rate rises, which cools demand and pushes inflation back down.

## Systematic Versus Unsystematic Monetary Policy (Complementary Lecture)

The complementary lecture formalizes a distinction that the rest of the course relies on:

- **Systematic policy** is the predictable response implied by the rule/framework.
- **Unsystematic policy** is the unexpected deviation from the rule, often written as a shock $\varepsilon_t^M$.

The complementary slides write the Taylor rule with a shock term:

$$
R_t = r_t^{LR} + \pi^* + 1.5(\pi_t - \pi^*) + 0.5(y_t - y_t^p) + \varepsilon_t^M
$$

Why this matters:

1. Expectations are forward-looking, so the systematic part of policy shapes behavior even before the instrument moves.
2. The shock part is useful for empirical work and model validation, but the complementary lecture stresses it typically explains a minor fraction of output fluctuations.

### Evidence block (what to remember)

The complementary lecture references Clarida, Gali and Gertler's estimated rules and a pre/post-1979 comparison.
The exam-relevant takeaway is not the estimation method. It is the interpretive point:

- systematic policy behavior can change across periods (and that can change macro stability)
- but there are caveats, including the "good luck" hypothesis and the fact that the Great Moderation ended with the 2008 crisis

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Mixing up objectives (inflation/employment outcomes) with the instrument (a policy rate) and the rule (the mapping from conditions to that rate).
- Treating "maximum employment" as a fixed numeric target rather than a concept measured with multiple indicators (slides).
- Forgetting that "output gap" can mean different things in different contexts (model gap vs empirical measure).
- Talking about "monetary policy shocks" as if they are the main content of policy rather than the unsystematic deviation from a systematic framework.

## Exam-Useful Takeaways

- You should be able to define objectives, instruments, and rules cleanly and give one concrete example of each from Lecture 1.
- You should be able to write the Taylor rule in its "level + gaps" form and explain what each term means in words.
- You should be able to explain the Taylor principle as a statement about how policy moves the real interest rate when inflation rises persistently.
- You should be able to distinguish systematic versus unsystematic policy and explain what a monetary policy shock $\varepsilon_t^M$ represents.
