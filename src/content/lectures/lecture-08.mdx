## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 8 shifts the course from representative-agent stabilization logic to heterogeneous-agent macro logic.
In earlier lectures you can often talk as if there is one "typical" household and one "typical" transmission mechanism.
Here the lecture forces you to separate two questions that get mixed up in representative-agent thinking:

1. **Incidence:** who gains and who loses from a shock or a policy move?
2. **Aggregation:** how do those gains/losses translate into total demand?

The slides emphasize a simple but powerful mechanism:

- households have different marginal propensities to consume (different MPCs)
- shocks and policy affect household incomes and balance sheets differently (different exposure)
- the aggregate effect depends on whether the high-MPC households are the households that are most affected

### Connection to the rest of the course

- Lectures 2 to 7: macro transmission is often summarized with real-rate gaps and expectations discipline in a baseline NK system.
- Lecture 8: redistribution becomes part of the transmission mechanism because "who moves" and "who spends" are not the same.
- Later lectures: this helps you write better policy answers because you can discuss both efficiency/stabilization and distribution/incidence clearly.

## Symbols, Parameters, and Objects (Keep These Straight)

Lecture 8 reuses a small set of objects. If you can define them cleanly, the matching-multiplier derivation is just algebra.

| Symbol | What it is in the lecture | How to say it on an exam |
|---|---|---|
| $C_i$ | consumption of household $i$ | "Household $i$'s spending." |
| $E_i$ | earnings / labor income of household $i$ | "Household $i$'s labor-income exposure to the cycle." |
| $C \\equiv \\sum_i C_i$ | aggregate consumption | "Total demand from consumption." |
| $Y \\equiv \\sum_i E_i$ | aggregate output/income proxy used in the slides | "The aggregate scale variable that earnings sum to." |
| $\\text{MPC}_i \\equiv \\frac{dC_i}{dE_i}$ | household $i$'s marginal propensity to consume out of earnings | "How much $i$ spends out of one extra unit of income." |
| $w_i \\equiv \\frac{E_i}{Y}$ | earnings weight | "Income-share weight used in the decomposition." |
| $x_i \\equiv \\frac{dE_i}{dY}\\frac{Y}{E_i}$ | cyclical exposure elasticity | "How cyclically sensitive $i$'s earnings are." |
| $\\text{MPC}^{agg} \\equiv \\frac{dC}{dY}$ | aggregate MPC / consumption sensitivity | "How strongly total consumption reacts when aggregate output/income changes." |

## Core Theory: Why Heterogeneity Changes Macro

The lecture gives a simple "history of thought" motivation for why modern macro moved beyond the representative-agent benchmark:

- first-generation benchmark: representative-agent macro (RBC/NK), useful for clear stabilization logic but silent on incidence
- second-generation heterogeneity: models mainly used for welfare/distribution questions, with limited aggregate feedback
- third-generation (post-crisis): richer two-way interaction where inequality can affect aggregate dynamics and policy transmission

The economic motivation in the slides is empirical and exam-relevant: growth, recessions, and policy effects are not evenly distributed.
So if high-MPC households are systematically the ones who get hit in downturns (or helped by stimulus), the aggregate response will differ from what a
representative-agent model would predict.

## MPC Heterogeneity (Why MPC_i Is the Key State Variable)

### Definition (used in the lecture)

The slides define the marginal propensity to consume as the fraction of an income (or earnings) change that shows up as consumption within a given horizon:

$$
\text{MPC}_i \equiv \frac{dC_i}{dY_i}
$$

Two important interpretation points (exam-safe):

- MPC is a *marginal* object (a derivative): it is about "one extra unit" of income, not average saving rates.
- MPC depends on the *horizon*: the lecture cites benchmark quarterly and annual MPC magnitudes to emphasize that short-run and longer-run responses differ.

### Why the distribution matters (not just the average)

The lecture contrast is:

- representative-agent models: one common MPC, typically low and closely tied to intertemporal substitution
- heterogeneous-agent models with incomplete markets: dispersed MPCs, often with a right tail of high-MPC households

So the key exam takeaway is not "MPC exists." It is: **MPC is distributed, and that distribution affects aggregates when shocks/policy redistribute income.**

### What drives MPC heterogeneity (intuition)

The slides list practical drivers. Translate them into mechanisms:

- liquid wealth / cash-on-hand: low liquid buffers tend to imply high MPCs
- illiquid wealth: you can be "wealthy on paper" but still have high MPC if wealth is hard to access quickly
- income risk / employment stability: higher risk raises the value of buffers and changes marginal spending behavior
- debt structure (fixed vs floating): determines how rate changes map into disposable income
- heterogeneity in preferences, patience, demographics: shifts desired saving and therefore marginal spending

## Step-By-Step Derivation: The Matching Multiplier (Why Covariance Matters)

This is the highest-yield piece of algebra in Lecture 8. It answers a precise question:

> When does inequality change aggregate demand responses, rather than only changing "who gets what"?

The lecture's answer is: when there is **matching** between MPC heterogeneity and cyclical exposure heterogeneity.

### Step 0 (Start from aggregate objects)

Define aggregates as sums of household objects:

$$
C = \sum_i C_i, \quad Y = \sum_i E_i
$$

Math move: define aggregates.

Economic meaning: total demand is built from many household spending decisions, and total income/output is built from many earnings streams.

### Step 1 (Differentiate aggregate consumption with respect to aggregate output)

The lecture's object of interest is:

$$
\text{MPC}^{agg} \equiv \frac{dC}{dY} = \sum_i \frac{dC_i}{dY}
$$

Math move: the derivative of a sum is the sum of derivatives.

Economic meaning: the aggregate response is the sum of all household responses.

### Step 2 (Chain rule: "MPC times exposure")

The slides then channel the effect through household earnings:

$$
\frac{dC_i}{dY} = \frac{dC_i}{dE_i}\frac{dE_i}{dY}
$$

so:

$$
\frac{dC}{dY} = \sum_i \frac{dC_i}{dE_i}\frac{dE_i}{dY}
$$

Math move: chain rule.

Economic meaning: a household matters for aggregate demand if it (i) has a high MPC and (ii) is actually hit when the aggregate moves.

### Step 3 (Rewrite using weights and cyclical exposure elasticities)

Define:

$$
w_i \equiv \frac{E_i}{Y},
\quad
x_i \equiv \frac{dE_i}{dY}\frac{Y}{E_i}
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{dE_i}{dY} = \frac{E_i}{Y}\frac{dE_i}{dY}\frac{Y}{E_i} = w_i x_i
$$

so the aggregate sensitivity can be written as:

$$
\text{MPC}^{agg}
= \sum_i w_i \underbrace{\left(\frac{dC_i}{dE_i}\right)}_{\text{MPC}_i}\underbrace{x_i}_{\text{exposure}}
$$

Math move: multiply and divide by $Y$ and $E_i$ to isolate a weight and an elasticity.

Economic meaning: the aggregate multiplier is larger when high-MPC households have high cyclical exposure.

### Step 4 (Decompose into "average MPC" plus a covariance term)

The lecture's slide writes the decomposition as:

$$
\text{MPC}^{agg}
=
\sum_i \frac{E_i}{Y}\frac{dC_i}{dE_i}
 \operatorname{cov}\!\left(
\frac{dC_i}{dE_i},
\frac{dE_i}{dY}\frac{Y}{E_i}
\right)
$$

Interpretation (what to say in words):

- first term: earnings-weighted average MPC
- second term: a matching/covariance term that is positive if high-MPC households are also the households with high exposure $x_i$

Intuition check: if exposure is proportional (roughly $x_i = 1$ for everyone), then the covariance term is zero, and the aggregate response collapses to the
earnings-weighted average MPC.

The slide reports calibration-style magnitudes from Patterson's framework that are there to make the point quantitative: the covariance term and nonlinear MPCs
can raise the effective aggregate multiplier relative to a representative-agent benchmark.

## Two-Way Link: Macro and Inequality

The lecture uses the phrase "macro matters for inequality and inequality matters for macro" in a precise sense:

- macro -> inequality: recessions, recoveries, and policy moves redistribute earnings and employment outcomes across households and groups
- inequality -> macro: aggregate demand depends on who is constrained/high-MPC versus who is wealthy/low-MPC, so the same aggregate shock can produce different
  demand responses in economies with different distributions

Exam-safe interpretation: this is not just "inequality is important." It is **two-way causality**: the distribution shapes aggregates and aggregates reshape the
distribution.

## Monetary Policy Transmission with Redistribution Channels (Auclert Intuition)

In Lectures 2 to 7 you can often tell a clean story: policy changes the real interest rate, which shifts demand through intertemporal substitution.
Lecture 8 adds an exam-relevant refinement: the policy move also redistributes resources across households, and those redistribution effects can feed back into
aggregate demand because MPCs differ.

### The incidence channels emphasized in the lecture

The slides highlight that an expansionary shock (higher income, higher inflation, lower real rates) affects households differently through channels such as:

- earnings heterogeneity channel (labor income exposure differs across households)
- Fisher channel (unexpected inflation changes real values of nominal assets/liabilities)
- interest-rate exposure channel (differences in maturity structure and repricing of assets/liabilities)

### HANK versus RANK reading of the consumption response

The source slide summarizes the consumption response as:

$$
\Delta C = \text{direct real-rate effect} + \text{indirect income/distribution effects}
$$

Lecture interpretation (exam-safe):

- RANK benchmark: the direct intertemporal-substitution channel is the core story.
- HANK benchmark: indirect income/distribution channels can be quantitatively important because incidence interacts with the MPC distribution.

## Evidence and Policy Strategy (How the Lecture Uses It)

The lecture uses evidence and institutional strategy to make the conceptual point concrete: distributional responses are not a footnote to policy, and in some
contexts they can change aggregate transmission.

### Evidence block (what to remember)

The slides cite evidence (for example the Coglianese-Olsson-Patterson slide) that monetary-policy shocks can generate heterogeneous unemployment/earnings
responses across the earnings distribution.

Exam-safe takeaway: contractionary policy can have uneven labor-market incidence, and that incidence can matter for aggregate demand when MPCs differ.

### Fed 2020 review (how it is used in the lecture)

The Fed section is used to connect the heterogeneous-incidence perspective to real-world strategy language:

- more explicit focus on broad-based and inclusive employment outcomes
- wording shift toward employment shortfalls
- average-inflation-targeting logic after prolonged undershooting

Exam framing: this is not "inequality replaces the inflation target." It is strategy design under low neutral rates, persistent undershooting risk, and uneven
labor-market outcomes.

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Saying "heterogeneity matters" without specifying which covariance/channel.
- Interpreting the matching term as "just correlation" without stating what is covarying (MPC with exposure) and what the weights are.
- Assuming borrowers and savers net out mechanically in policy transmission.
- Treating HANK as "RANK plus one extra parameter" instead of a distributional-state framework.
- Using reduced-form inequality facts without connecting them to a transmission mechanism.

## Exam-Useful Takeaways

- You should be able to define $\text{MPC}_i$ in words and explain why the **distribution** of MPCs matters for aggregates when shocks/policy redistribute income.
- You should be able to reproduce the chain-rule logic: aggregate demand responds through "MPC times exposure,"
  $$
  \frac{dC}{dY} = \sum_i \frac{dC_i}{dE_i}\frac{dE_i}{dY}.
  $$
- You should be able to state and interpret the decomposition into an average term plus a matching/covariance term, and explain what a positive covariance means
  economically (high-MPC households are also highly exposed).
- You should be able to name the redistribution channels emphasized in the lecture (earnings heterogeneity, Fisher, interest-rate exposure) and explain why they
  are part of transmission in a heterogeneous-agent view.
- You should be able to explain (at a high level) how the lecture links distributional evidence to strategy discussions like the Fed 2020 review without claiming
  that inflation targeting is abandoned.
