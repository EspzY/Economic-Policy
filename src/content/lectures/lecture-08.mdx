## Overview

Lecture 8 shifts the course from representative-agent stabilization logic to heterogeneous-agent macro logic.
The central mechanism in the source slides is simple:

1. households differ in how much they consume out of income changes (different MPCs)
2. shocks and policy do not hit all households equally
3. aggregate effects depend on whether high-MPC households are the ones most exposed

This is why inequality and macro are treated as two-way interactions, not separate topics.

## Part A: From representative-agent macro to heterogeneous-agent macro

The lecture gives a history-of-thought framing:

- first generation (roughly 1970-1990): representative-agent macro (RBC/NK benchmark)
- second generation (roughly 1990-pre-crisis): heterogeneous-agent models mainly used for distribution/welfare, with limited macro feedback
- third generation (post-crisis): richer two-way interaction where inequality can change aggregate dynamics and policy transmission

The economic motivation in the slides is empirical:

- growth and recessions are unequally distributed
- welfare/policy questions require "who gains, who loses?" analysis
- better data + better computation + post-crisis events increased the relevance of heterogeneity

## Part B: MPC as the key state variable for distributional macro

### B1. Definition used in the lecture

The slides define the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) as the share of a small, unexpected windfall spent within
the chosen horizon:

$$
\text{MPC}_i = \frac{dC_i}{dY_i}
$$

with reported benchmark moments around:

- quarterly average MPC around 0.25
- annual average MPC around 0.5

### B2. Why representative-agent logic misses this margin

The lecture contrasts:

- representative-agent models: one common MPC, often low and close to interest-rate-scale behavior
- heterogeneous-agent models with incomplete markets: dispersed MPCs and potentially large aggregate MPC

So the exam-relevant point is not "MPC exists", but "MPC is distributed".

### B3. What drives MPC heterogeneity

The slides list concrete drivers:

- employment stability and labor-income risk
- liquid wealth (cash-on-hand)
- illiquid wealth share (for example housing)
- debt structure (fixed versus flexible rates)
- demographics, preferences, and patience

## Part C: Step-by-step matching-multiplier logic (Patterson)

### C1. Start from aggregate consumption sensitivity

Define aggregate consumption and output:

$$
C = \sum_i C_i, \quad Y = \sum_i E_i
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{dC}{dY} = \sum_i \frac{dC_i}{dY}
           = \sum_i \frac{dC_i}{dE_i}\frac{dE_i}{dY}
$$

This already shows the key interaction: household spending sensitivity times household earnings exposure to aggregate conditions.

### C2. Decomposition emphasized in the slides

The lecture writes this as a weighted-average term plus a covariance term:

$$
\text{MPC}^{agg}
=
\sum_i \frac{E_i}{Y}\frac{dC_i}{dE_i}
\,
\operatorname{cov}\!\left(
\frac{dC_i}{dE_i},
\frac{dE_i}{dY}\frac{Y}{E_i}
\right)
$$

Interpretation:

- first term: average MPC (earnings-weighted)
- second term: alignment between "high MPC" and "high cyclical earnings exposure"

### C3. Economic meaning: the matching multiplier

The lecture's matching-multiplier idea is:

- if high-MPC households are also most exposed in downturns, aggregate responses are amplified
- if exposure mainly falls on low-MPC households, amplification is weaker

The slide reports calibration-style magnitudes from Patterson's framework:

- heterogeneous cyclical exposure raises MPC by about 7 percentage points
- adding nonlinear MPC effects raises total multiplier increase to about 17 percentage points

## Part D: Why inequality and macro move both ways

Using the "When Inequality Matters for Macro and Macro Matters for Inequality" discussion in the lecture:

- macro -> inequality: aggregate shocks reallocate outcomes across households and skill groups
- inequality -> macro: consumption dynamics depend on the share and behavior of hand-to-mouth/high-MPC households

That is the exam-safe meaning of `inequality <-> macro`: two-direction causality, not a one-way distributional add-on.

## Part E: Monetary policy transmission with redistribution channels

### E1. Auclert channel decomposition

The lecture highlights that an expansionary shock (higher income, higher inflation, lower real rates) affects households differently via:

- earnings heterogeneity channel
- Fisher channel (inflation and nominal positions)
- interest-rate exposure channel

So redistribution is part of transmission, not a side effect after transmission.

### E2. HANK versus RANK reading of monetary policy

The source slide summarizes:

$$
\Delta C = \text{direct real-rate effect} + \text{indirect income/distribution effects}
$$

Lecture interpretation:

- RANK benchmark: direct intertemporal-substitution channel dominates
- HANK benchmark: direct channel smaller; indirect income/distribution channels are larger and depend on MPC and asset structure

This is why heterogeneity can change both policy multipliers and incidence.

## Part F: Empirical and policy-strategy implications

### F1. Empirical labor-market evidence block

The Coglianese-Olsson-Patterson slide points to heterogeneous unemployment/earnings responses across the earnings distribution after monetary shocks.
The lecture takeaway is that contractionary policy can have larger distributional effects than representative-agent language suggests.

### F2. Fed 2020 review link used in the lecture

The Fed section is used to connect theory to central-bank strategy:

- more explicit focus on broad-based and inclusive employment outcomes
- wording shift toward employment shortfalls
- average-inflation-targeting logic after prolonged undershooting

Exam framing:

- this is not "inequality replaces inflation target"
- it is strategy design under low neutral rates, persistent undershooting risk, and distributionally uneven labor-market outcomes

## Common pitfalls

- Saying "heterogeneity matters" without specifying which covariance/channel.
- Assuming borrowers and savers net out mechanically in policy transmission.
- Treating HANK as "RANK plus one extra parameter" instead of a distributional-state framework.
- Using reduced-form inequality facts without connecting them to a transmission mechanism.
