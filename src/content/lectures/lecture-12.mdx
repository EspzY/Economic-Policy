## Overview

Lecture 12 is the course's "big picture" policy finale. The slide deck covers:

- definition of sustainable development (Brundtland)
- growth with (and without) resource constraints
- externalities, the Coase theorem, and basic welfare-theorem language
- climate change as a global flow-stock externality with uncertainty and ethical/discounting issues
- renewable and non-renewable resource economics (including the Hotelling rule)
- a brief link to international policy (Paris agreement) and UN sustainable development goals

This module is written to be exam-ready: each block ends with a "what you can say in words" interpretation.

## Part A: What "sustainability" means here

The slides quote the Brundtland report definition:

> Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

Economic translation:

- intertemporal constraints matter (stocks, not only flows)
- policies are evaluated on long horizons (future welfare, not only current output)
- distribution across generations is part of the problem (fairness and discounting)

## Part B: Growth, resources, and the "drag" from constraints

### B1. Baseline Solow structure (from the slides)

The lecture starts from the Solow model:

$$
Y(t) = F(K(t), A(t)L(t))
$$

with population and productivity growth:

$$
\dot L = nL, \quad \dot A = gA
$$

and capital accumulation:

$$
\dot K = sY - \delta K
$$

The key slide takeaway: in steady state, output per capita growth is pinned down by productivity growth $g$.

### B2. Extend production with resources and land

The deck then extends the production function to include a resource input $R$ and land/terrain $T$:

$$
Y = K^{\alpha} R^{\beta} T^{\gamma} (AL)^{1-\alpha-\beta-\gamma}
$$

The log-derivative accounting identity is:

$$
\frac{\dot Y}{Y}=
\alpha\frac{\dot K}{K}+\beta\frac{\dot R}{R}+\gamma\frac{\dot T}{T}
(1-\alpha-\beta-\gamma)\left(\frac{\dot A}{A}+\frac{\dot L}{L}\right)
$$

The slide considers constraint cases like:

- non-renewable resource depletion: $\dot R/R=-b$
- fixed land: $\dot T/T=0$

In a balanced-growth style steady state where $K$ and $Y$ grow at the same rate, the lecture solves a growth rate of:

$$
g_Y = \frac{-\beta b + (1-\alpha-\beta-\gamma)(g+n)}{1-\alpha}
$$

Interpretation: resource depletion and fixed factors reduce growth relative to the baseline, and the magnitude of the
drag increases with factor shares and with population growth.

## Part C: Externalities, Coase, and welfare-theorem language

The slides place environmental policy inside standard market-failure logic:

- externality: social costs differ from private costs
- policy tools: taxes, subsidies, quotas, and property-right design (Coase)
- problems differ by whether they are global/local and stock/flow, and by distributional consequences

### C1. Coase theorem (as stated on the slide)

The slide statement is:

- when externalities exist, parties can sometimes bargain to internalize them and reach efficiency

Exam nuance: the theorem is a *conditional* claim (property rights, low transaction costs, and the ability to bargain).

### C2. Welfare theorems (as on the slide)

- 1st welfare theorem: competitive markets reach a Pareto efficient allocation (under strong assumptions)
- 2nd welfare theorem: with appropriate redistribution, any Pareto efficient allocation can be supported by markets

This is why distributional issues and efficiency issues are separated (conceptually) but interact in practice.

The slide bullets emphasize:

- global origins and global impacts
- long horizons and flow-stock processes

### D1. Uncertainty and catastrophic risk

The lecture's uncertainty slide notes:

- current concentration around 430 ppm
- uncertainty about effects
- very large temperature increases are catastrophic

The included readings make the same theme explicit:

- Stern frames GHG emissions as a global externality and emphasizes risk and uncertainty.
- Weitzman focuses on low-probability, high-impact outcomes ("fat tails") and how this can change the role of discounting.

### D2. Discounting and fairness

The slide calls out the basic issue:

- discounting reduces the weight on future costs and benefits
- fairness across generations is therefore unavoidable in climate analysis

Exam framing: explain discounting as a normative choice (not only a technical parameter) when costs and benefits are intergenerational.

### D3. Paris agreement bullets in the slides

The deck mentions:

- adopted by many countries in 2015 with temperature goals around 2C (with 1.5C as preferred)
- recurring cycles of nationally determined contributions (NDCs)
- practical constraints like finance, technology, and capacity

## Part E: Public goods and the commons (why Hardin is relevant)

The slides include the standard classification by rivalry and excludability:

- private goods (rival, excludable)
- club goods (non-rival, excludable)
- common goods (rival, non-excludable)
- public goods (non-rival, non-excludable)

Hardin's "Tragedy of the Commons" reading is the canonical illustration of why common goods can be overused when
individual incentives do not internalize the social cost.

## Part F: Resource economics (renewable vs non-renewable)

The lecture separates:

- renewable-resource extraction with property-right problems
- non-renewable-resource depletion where scarcity rent matters over time

### F1. Hotelling rule for non-renewable resources (as shown on the slide)

The slide writes the key condition:

$$
\frac{\dot p}{p} = r
$$

One way to see the logic is the standard present-value optimization:

- choose an extraction path $x(t)$
- total stock constraint: $\int_0^\infty x(t)\,dt = S_0$
- maximize present value: $\int_0^\infty p(t)x(t)e^{-rt}\,dt$

The first-order condition implies $p(t)e^{-rt}$ is constant over time, which is equivalent to $\dot p/p=r$.

Interpretation:

- resource price rises at the interest rate (scarcity rent increases over time)
- the timing of extraction is about arbitrage between selling today and selling later

## Common pitfalls

- Saying "sustainability" as a slogan without stating the intertemporal constraint.
- Treating climate change like a local flow externality (it is global and stock-based in the slides).
- Using Coase as a universal solution without mentioning transaction costs and institutions.
- Mixing renewable and non-renewable logic (the policy problems differ).
- Treating discounting as purely technical when the main issue is intergenerational fairness.
