## Overview

Lecture 11 is about what "inequality" means in macro policy discussions and how to measure it.
The lecture separates two uses:

1. functional distribution: labor share versus capital share
2. personal distribution: inequality between individuals/households

It then links wage-setting institutions (Scandinavian model) to inflation dynamics and closes with measurement tools
(Lorenz curves, Gini coefficient, and basic axioms).

## Part A: Two inequality concepts (do not mix them)

### A1. Functional distribution

Functional distribution is about how national income is split between factors:

- labor income (wages)
- capital income (rents/profits/user cost of capital)

### A2. Personal distribution

Personal distribution is about the dispersion of income across people:

- bottom versus top shares
- Lorenz curve comparisons
- scalar indices like the Gini coefficient

Exam rule: always state which concept you are using before making claims about policy.

## Part B: Functional distribution from marginal products

The lecture uses the neoclassical theory of distribution:

- each factor is paid its marginal product
- this is a starting point, not the full institutional story

### B1. Production function and factor prices

Slides use:

$$
Y = A F(K,L)
$$

With price level $P$, wages and capital cost satisfy:

$$
W = P A \frac{\partial F(K,L)}{\partial L}
$$

$$
R = P A \frac{\partial F(K,L)}{\partial K}
$$

### B2. Constant returns and factor shares

Under constant returns to scale, Euler's theorem implies total income can be decomposed into factor payments.
That is the mathematical basis for labor share and capital share calculations in the slides.

Interpretation:

- changes in $A$, $K/L$, and technology can shift marginal products
- institutions and bargaining can shift observed wage shares away from the benchmark

## Part C: Wage setting and the Scandinavian model of inflation

This part of the lecture is about how wage formation in a small open economy can translate into inflation dynamics.
The slides describe two sectors:

- traded sector ($t$)
- non-traded sector ($n$)

Key objects are growth rates (dot notation in the slides). The block shown:

$$
\dot p^t = \dot e + \dot p^x
$$

$$
\dot w = \dot p^t + \dot a^t
$$

$$
\dot p^n = \dot w - \dot a^n
$$

and CPI inflation as a weighted average:

$$
\dot p = \alpha \dot p^n + (1-\alpha)\dot p^t
$$

### C1. Fixed exchange rates

Under a fixed exchange-rate regime, the exchange-rate growth term is treated as given and the model implies:

$$
\dot p = \dot p^x + \alpha(\dot a^t - \dot a^n)
$$

The lecture calls this a Balassa-Samuelson type mechanism: higher productivity growth in traded sectors can raise
overall inflation through relative price adjustment.

### C2. Inflation targeting

Under inflation targeting, the slides solve for the exchange-rate term consistent with the inflation target:

$$
\dot e = \dot p^{target} - \dot p^x - \alpha(\dot a^t - \dot a^n)
$$

The deck then adds "complications" bullets:

- interest response by the central bank
- production and unemployment effects

### C3. Wage negotiations 2024 slide (policy interpretation)

The lecture notes:

- wage share has fallen a lot in the traded sector
- restoring wage share can boost inflation via the non-traded sector
- policy response may involve higher interest rates

Exam style: connect wage setting to inflation transmission before discussing what the central bank might do.

## Part D: Personal distribution and the role of policy

The slide on personal income distribution emphasizes that observed personal inequality is shaped by:

- market outcomes
- taxes
- transfers
- government services

So policy debates must specify whether they refer to pre-tax/pre-transfer or post-tax/post-transfer concepts.

## Part E: Inequality axioms used in the lecture

The inequality-index slide writes an index as a function of an income vector $(y_1,\dots,y_n)$ and lists criteria.
The key ones are:

- anonymity principle: the index should not depend on labels, only on the multiset of incomes
- population principle: duplicating the population should not change the index
- scale/relative income principle: scaling all incomes by a constant should not change relative inequality
- Pigou-Dalton transfer principle: transferring income from richer to poorer (without reversing ranks) should reduce inequality

These criteria help you judge if a proposed inequality measure is sensible.

## Part F: Lorenz curves and scalar indices

### F1. Lorenz curve definition (as in the slides)

The Lorenz curve is constructed by:

1. sorting the population from lowest to highest income
2. plotting cumulative population share on the horizontal axis
3. plotting cumulative income share on the vertical axis

The 45-degree line represents perfect equality. Greater inequality means the Lorenz curve lies further below that line.

### F2. Lorenz consistency and crossing

The slides note:

- an inequality measure is Lorenz consistent if it ranks distributions consistently with Lorenz dominance
- Lorenz curves can cross, so not all comparisons are unambiguous

Exam-safe statement: if Lorenz curves cross, you must say which inequality measure you use and why.

### F3. Gini coefficient (two equivalent interpretations)

The slides describe the Gini coefficient as:

- the ratio of the area between the equality line and the Lorenz curve to the total area under the equality line
- equivalently, a normalization of the mean difference across all income pairs

A standard representation consistent with that slide description is:

$$
G = \frac{1}{2n^2\mu}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\lvert y_i - y_k\rvert
$$

where $\mu$ is average income.

## Common pitfalls

- Talking about "inequality" without stating personal versus functional meaning.
- Treating the neoclassical distribution benchmark as an institutional fact rather than a baseline model result.
- Forgetting that Scandinavian wage-setting logic depends on traded versus non-traded structure.
- Treating Gini as a complete summary even when Lorenz curves cross.
