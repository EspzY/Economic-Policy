## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 4 introduced a genuine stabilization trade-off (cost-push shocks). Lecture 5 is about what happens once you let
policy influence the economy through **promises** and **expectations**.

In the New Keynesian framework, commitment is not an abstract moral virtue. It is a technical statement:
the central bank can credibly choose a *policy plan* (or a rule) that shapes private expectations today.
That typically improves outcomes relative to discretion, where the policymaker re-optimizes period by period.

The slides treat two layers of commitment:

1. commitment to a **simple targeting-rule family** (choose the best coefficient within a restricted class)
2. **full state-contingent commitment** (choose complete sequences, state by state)

Lecture 5 is also the bridge to Lecture 6: if commitment improves outcomes, you must ask why it might be difficult to sustain (time inconsistency) and what institutions can help.

## Reference Point: Discretion (Lecture 4)

Under discretion, the Lecture 4 targeting condition is static:

$$
x_t = -\frac{\kappa}{\alpha_x}\pi_t.
$$

Economic meaning: the policymaker trades off inflation and the welfare gap **within the period**, taking expectations as given.
There is no "memory" term (no $x_{t-1}$) in the condition.

## Commitment I: Commitment To A Simple Targeting-Rule Family

The slides ask: suppose the central bank can commit, but only to a rule of the same functional form as discretion:

$$
x_t = -\frac{\kappa}{\alpha_x^c}\pi_t.
$$

The policymaker now chooses $\alpha_x^c$ to minimize the discounted loss.
The headline result in the slides is:

$$
\alpha_x^c = \alpha_x(1-\beta\rho_{\pi}) < \alpha_x.
$$

Interpretation in one sentence: with commitment, policy behaves as if it places a *lower effective weight* on output-gap stabilization in the targeting rule, because credibility improves the inflation-output trade-off via expectations.

### Step-by-step derivation of $\alpha_x^c = \alpha_x(1-\beta\rho_{\pi})$ (Slides)

The slides provide the equilibrium responses under this simple-rule commitment class:

$$
\pi_t = \alpha_x^c\Psi^c u_t^{\pi}, \quad x_t = -\kappa\Psi^c u_t^{\pi},
\quad
\Psi^c = \frac{1}{\kappa^2 + \alpha_x^c(1-\beta\rho_{\pi})}.
$$

**Step 1 (Write per-period loss under the rule)**

Start from the (Lecture 4) per-period loss:

$$
\ell_t = \frac{1}{2}\left(\pi_t^2 + \alpha_x x_t^2\right).
$$

Insert the response formulas (both $\pi_t$ and $x_t$ are proportional to $u_t^{\pi}$):

$$
\ell_t
= \frac{1}{2}(\Psi^c)^2\left((\alpha_x^c)^2 + \alpha_x\kappa^2\right)\left(u_t^{\pi}\right)^2.
$$

Math move: factor out $(\Psi^c u_t^{\pi})^2$.

Economic meaning: under a linear rule, the shock $u_t^{\pi}$ is the state, and policy chooses a coefficient that determines how much inflation and the welfare gap move per unit of the shock.

**Step 2 (Reduce the choice of $\alpha_x^c$ to a coefficient minimization problem)**

Up to a constant scaling, minimizing discounted loss over $\alpha_x^c$ is equivalent to minimizing:

$$
F(\alpha_x^c) \equiv
\frac{(\alpha_x^c)^2 + \alpha_x\kappa^2}{\left(\kappa^2 + \alpha_x^c(1-\beta\rho_{\pi})\right)^2}.
$$

This is exactly the object shown in the slide’s derivative steps.

**Step 3 (Differentiate and solve)**

Let $A \equiv (1-\beta\rho_{\pi})$ and $D(\alpha) \equiv \kappa^2 + \alpha A$.
Then:

$$
F(\alpha) = \frac{\alpha^2 + \alpha_x\kappa^2}{D(\alpha)^2}.
$$

Differentiate:

$$
F'(\alpha) = 0
\;\Longleftrightarrow\;
2\alpha D(\alpha) - 2(\alpha^2 + \alpha_x\kappa^2)A = 0.
$$

Divide by $2$ and substitute $D(\alpha)=\kappa^2+\alpha A$:

$$
\alpha(\kappa^2+\alpha A) = (\alpha^2 + \alpha_x\kappa^2)A.
$$

Cancel the $\alpha^2 A$ terms on both sides:

$$
\alpha\kappa^2 = \alpha_x\kappa^2 A.
$$

Since $\kappa^2>0$, conclude:

$$
\alpha_x^c = \alpha_x(1-\beta\rho_{\pi}).
$$

Economic meaning: persistence ($\rho_{\pi}$) and patience ($\beta$) raise the value of being credible. When $\beta\rho_{\pi}$ is high, commitment makes it optimal to act more aggressively against inflationary wedges.

## Commitment II: Full State-Contingent Commitment (History Dependence)

The second block removes the simple-rule restriction and lets the central bank choose full state-contingent sequences
for future inflation and output gaps.

The commitment note (Sveen) writes the commitment Lagrangian (schematically) as a discounted sum of:

- the quadratic loss in $(\pi_t,x_t)$
- plus multipliers on the NKPC constraints in each future period/state

The key is that with forward-looking constraints, the multiplier becomes a state variable: past promises affect today’s FOCs.

### Step-by-step derivation of the history-dependent targeting relation (Slides + Sveen note)

The slide deck’s commitment-II first-order conditions can be summarized as two relationships.

**Step 1 (FOC with respect to $x_t$ gives a multiplier-output link)**

From the FOC in the notes/slides, you obtain:

$$
\lambda_t = \frac{\alpha_x}{\kappa}x_t.
$$

Math move: the multiplier scales the marginal cost of relaxing the NKPC constraint; with a quadratic loss, it is proportional to $x_t$.

Economic meaning: if policy is pushing $x_t$ far from efficient output, the shadow value of the Phillips-curve constraint becomes large.

**Step 2 (FOC with respect to $\pi_t$ creates multiplier dynamics)**

Because inflation appears in the NKPC both today and (discounted) tomorrow, the FOC links adjacent multipliers:

$$
\lambda_t = \lambda_{t-1} - \pi_t,
\qquad
\lambda_{-1}=0.
$$

Math move: the lagged multiplier appears because commitment treats the entire planned path consistently across time.

Economic meaning: if inflation is above target today, the promised plan "builds up" a state variable that forces future policy to lean the other way.
This is the origin of history dependence.

**Step 3 (Combine to obtain the history-dependent targeting rule)**

Substitute $\lambda_t = (\alpha_x/\kappa)x_t$ into the multiplier-dynamics equation:

$$
\frac{\alpha_x}{\kappa}x_t = \frac{\alpha_x}{\kappa}x_{t-1} - \pi_t
\Rightarrow
x_t - x_{t-1} = -\frac{\kappa}{\alpha_x}\pi_t.
$$

This is the core history-dependence condition highlighted in the lecture.

### Interpretation: why this looks like “memory” (and why it helps)

Compare with discretion:

- discretion: $x_t$ is tied to $\pi_t$ *within the period*
- commitment: the change in $x_t$ is tied to $\pi_t$, so yesterday matters

This "memory" is not decoration. It is the mechanism through which commitment changes expectations and improves the trade-off.

## Implementation Perspective (Why Institutions Start To Matter)

The interest-rate note for discretion already emphasizes a gap between:

- describing an optimal allocation in terms of $(\pi_t,x_t)$
- and writing an implementable instrument rule that uniquely generates it

Under commitment, the implementation problem remains, and the history dependence adds further realism constraints.
This is why policy credibility and institutions naturally become central in Lecture 6.

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Saying commitment means "lower inflation today" without discussing expectations and the promised future path.
- Ignoring the role of shock persistence ($\rho_{\pi}$) in the gain from commitment.
- Confusing "commitment to a simple rule family" with "full state-contingent commitment."
- Forgetting that history dependence comes from the intertemporal first-order conditions (the multiplier dynamics), not from ad hoc inertia.
