## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 5 showed that commitment can improve outcomes because expectations are part of the transmission mechanism.
Lecture 6 answers the natural next question:

> If commitment is better, why can’t policymakers just announce the best policy and then do it?

The lecture’s answer is **time inconsistency**: an action that is optimal to promise ex ante may not be optimal to carry out ex post.
Private expectations anticipate that temptation, and equilibrium outcomes can be worse even when policymakers have good intentions.

This lecture is exam-high-yield because it trains you to:

- separate **temptation** (one-period incentive to deviate) from **equilibrium** (what happens when expectations adjust)
- do a clean substitution/FOC derivation
- interpret independence as an institutional commitment device rather than a slogan

## Symbols, Parameters, And Assumptions

The time-inconsistency block in the slides uses a compact Romer/Barro-Gordon style representation.

| Object | Meaning | Note |
|---|---|---|
| $\pi$ | inflation | policy variable in the simplified problem |
| $\pi^*$ | desired/target inflation | the benchmark the policymaker would like under commitment |
| $\pi^e$ | expected inflation | set by the private sector before policy is chosen under discretion |
| $y$ | output | realized after policy/expectations |
| $y^n$ | natural output | output when there is no surprise inflation |
| $y^*$ | desired output level | the political/ambition benchmark; the key assumption is $y^* > y^n$ |
| $a>0$ | output stabilization weight | how much the policymaker dislikes output deviations |
| $b>0$ | slope of expectations-augmented supply | how much surprise inflation moves output in the short run |

## Core Mechanism (Intuition Before Algebra)

The model has two ingredients:

1. **A short-run temptation:** if $y^* > y^n$, policymakers would like output above the natural level. In the short run, surprise inflation can move output (the supply relation).
2. **Rational expectations discipline:** if policymakers repeatedly have that temptation, private agents will anticipate it, raising expected inflation. That destroys the systematic output gain.

So the time-inconsistency result is not "policymakers are irrational." It is: incentives plus expectations produce an equilibrium with **inflation bias** and no sustained output gain.

## Step-By-Step Derivation: Discretionary Policy And Inflation Bias

### Step 1 (Policy objective in the slides)

The lecture uses:

$$
L = \frac{1}{2}\left[(\pi-\pi^*)^2 + a(y-y^*)^2\right], \quad a>0.
$$

Math move: write a quadratic loss in inflation deviations and output deviations.

Economic meaning: the policymaker dislikes inflation away from target and dislikes output away from a desired level $y^*$.
The key time-inconsistency assumption is $y^* > y^n$ (ambition above the natural level).

### Step 2 (Supply relation and substitution)

The expectations-augmented supply relation is:

$$
y - y^n = b(\pi-\pi^e), \quad b>0.
$$

Solve for $y$:

$$
y = y^n + b(\pi-\pi^e).
$$

Math move: rewrite output as natural output plus a surprise-inflation term.

Economic meaning: only **surprise** inflation moves output; if $\pi=\pi^e$, output is at $y^n$.

### Step 3 (Rewrite the loss purely in terms of π)

Compute:

$$
y-y^* = (y^n-y^*) + b(\pi-\pi^e).
$$

Insert into the objective:

$$
L(\pi;\pi^e)
= \frac{1}{2}\left[(\pi-\pi^*)^2 + a\left((y^n-y^*) + b(\pi-\pi^e)\right)^2\right].
$$

Math move: the discretionary problem is now "choose $\pi$ taking $\pi^e$ as given."

Economic meaning: under discretion, the policymaker treats expectations as predetermined in the current period.

### Step 4 (FOC under discretion)

Differentiate with respect to $\pi$:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \pi}
= (\pi-\pi^*) + ab\left((y^n-y^*) + b(\pi-\pi^e)\right) = 0.
$$

Expand and collect terms:

$$
(\pi-\pi^*) + ab(y^n-y^*) + ab^2(\pi-\pi^e) = 0
$$

$$
(1+ab^2)\pi = \pi^* + ab^2\pi^e + ab(y^*-y^n).
$$

This matches the expression highlighted in the lecture slides.

### Step 5 (Rational expectations equilibrium)

Under rational expectations, $\pi^e=\pi$ in equilibrium. Substitute into the discretionary solution:

$$
(1+ab^2)\pi = \pi^* + ab^2\pi + ab(y^*-y^n)
\Rightarrow
\pi = \pi^* + ab(y^*-y^n).
$$

Economic meaning: the equilibrium inflation rate is above target when policymakers want output above natural level.
That is the **inflation bias**.

Now use the supply relation. Since $\pi=\pi^e$ in equilibrium:

$$
y-y^n = b(\pi-\pi^e)=0 \Rightarrow y=y^n.
$$

Economic meaning: the private sector is not systematically fooled. The output gain disappears in equilibrium.

## Commitment Benchmark (Why Credibility Removes Inflation Bias)

Under credible commitment, the benchmark outcome in this simplified setup is:

$$
\pi^C = \pi^*, \quad y=y^n.
$$

Economic meaning: if policy can credibly anchor expectations at the target, there is no equilibrium incentive problem that generates persistent inflation above target.

## Central-Bank Independence As An Institutional Commitment Device

The lecture frames independence as a mechanism that can push discretionary outcomes closer to commitment outcomes by reducing short-term political pressure.
Institutional channels noted in the slides include:

- legal mandates
- appointment structures and tenure beyond political cycles
- targeting frameworks that strengthen credibility

Economic meaning: independence is a way to change incentives and credibility, not a way to remove shocks or delete trade-offs.

## Evidence Block (How To Use It In Exam Answers)

The slides reference both classic cross-country evidence (independence linked to better inflation outcomes) and newer evidence/papers on political pressure and credibility channels.
On the exam, the safe way to use the evidence is:

1. state the theory mechanism (temptation + expectations → inflation bias)
2. state what the evidence is consistent with (pressure/credibility affecting inflation outcomes)
3. avoid turning the evidence into a list of citations without a mechanism

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Treating the problem as only "high inflation" rather than a credibility/expectations equilibrium problem.
- Confusing the one-period temptation (given $\pi^e$) with the rational-expectations equilibrium outcome.
- Forgetting that the inflation-bias result depends on the assumption $y^*>y^n$.
- Treating independence as magic rather than as an institutional commitment device.

- Write the objective and supply relation, and explain in words why $y^*>y^n$ creates temptation.
