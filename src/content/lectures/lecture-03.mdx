## Big Picture (Why This Lecture Matters)

Lecture 2 gave you the *ingredients* of the baseline New Keynesian (NK) model. Lecture 3 turns those ingredients into a
*solvable system* and then uses that solution discipline to teach a key benchmark: **divine coincidence**.

If you really learn Lecture 3 (not just the headlines), you gain three exam-safe skills:

1. You can show *algebraically* why the NKPC is forward-looking (recursive substitution + transversality).
2. You can explain when an interest-rate rule delivers a **unique** equilibrium and when it permits **sunspot** equilibria.
3. You can explain what “strict inflation targeting is optimal” means *and* why implementability is not automatic.

### How it connects to the rest of the course

- Lecture 3 is the clean baseline where “targets line up.”
- Lecture 4 breaks the clean result by adding a wedge (cost-push shocks), which reintroduces a real trade-off.
- Later lectures reuse the same determinacy/expectations logic when discussing commitment, discretion, and institutions.

## Symbols And Parameters (Read This Before The Algebra)

The course uses model-defined objects that are easy to confuse with empirical counterparts. The output gap here is a
**model wedge**, not a trend deviation from data.

### Endogenous variables (solved inside the system)

**Inflation $\pi_t$**: defined as $\pi_t \equiv p_t - p_{t-1}$ in the slides.

**Output gap $\tilde{y}_t$**: $\tilde{y}_t \equiv y_t - y_t^n$.
Economic meaning: “how far output is from the flexible-price benchmark” (the welfare-relevant tightness measure).

**Nominal policy rate $i_t$**: the instrument in the Taylor-type rule.

### Exogenous processes / shocks

**Natural real rate $r_t^n$**: the real rate consistent with flexible prices (a model concept; used to define “neutral” policy).

**Policy shock $v_t$**: disturbance in the interest-rate rule.

**Technology $a_t$ and demand/discount $z_t$**: shocks used in the slide’s expression for $r_t^n$.

### Key parameters

**$\beta \in (0,1)$**: discount factor; also the weight on expected future inflation in NKPC.

**$\sigma>0$**: governs how sensitive demand is to the real-rate gap in DIS.

**$\kappa>0$**: NKPC slope; how strongly the output gap moves inflation.

**$\phi_\pi,\phi_y$**: policy-rule feedback strengths on inflation and output gap.

## The Baseline 3-Equation NK Model (And What Each Equation “Does”)

The slide deck writes the baseline block as:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa \tilde{y}_t
$$

$$
\tilde{y}_t = E_t \tilde{y}_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}\left(i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1} - r_t^n\right)
$$

$$
i_t = \rho + \phi_{\pi}\pi_t + \phi_y \tilde{y}_t + v_t
$$

Translate each line into words before you touch algebra:

- **NKPC:** inflation depends on expected future inflation and real pressure (the output gap).
- **DIS:** demand depends on expected future demand and on the real rate relative to the natural real rate.
- **Rule:** the policy rate reacts to inflation and the gap (or it can fail to, which matters for determinacy).

## Derivation 1: Recursive Substitution For The NKPC (No Skipped Steps)

The lecture’s key claim is: **the baseline NKPC is purely forward-looking** (no direct role for lagged inflation).
The provided `recursive_substitution.pdf` shows the algebra. Below is the same derivation, but with explicit explanation at each move.

### Step 0 (Starting point)

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa \tilde{y}_t
$$

Math move: write the NKPC at time $t$.

Economic meaning: inflation today is partly expectations-driven ($E_t\pi_{t+1}$) and partly driven by real pressure ($\tilde{y}_t$).

### Step 1 (Write the NKPC one period ahead)

At $t+1$ the same NKPC holds:

$$
\pi_{t+1} = \beta E_{t+1}\pi_{t+2} + \kappa \tilde{y}_{t+1}
$$

Math move: shift the same equation forward in time.

Economic meaning: tomorrow’s inflation will depend on tomorrow’s expectations and tomorrow’s output gap.

### Step 2 (Take expectations at time t)

Apply $E_t[\cdot]$ to the $t+1$ equation:

$$
E_t\pi_{t+1} = \beta E_t\pi_{t+2} + \kappa E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}
$$

Math move: use iterated expectations: $E_t(E_{t+1}[\pi_{t+2}]) = E_t[\pi_{t+2}]$.

Economic meaning: what matters for today’s inflation is what agents expect today about future inflation and future gaps.

### Step 3 (Substitute back into the original NKPC)

Insert Step 2 into Step 0:

$$
\pi_t = \beta(\beta E_t\pi_{t+2} + \kappa E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}) + \kappa \tilde{y}_t
$$

so:

$$
\pi_t = \beta^2 E_t\pi_{t+2} + \kappa \tilde{y}_t + \kappa\beta E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}
$$

Math move: substitution plus collecting terms.

Economic meaning: inflation today loads on expected future gaps because firms price today based on expected future conditions.

### Step 4 (Iterate forward T times)

Continuing the same substitution logic yields the finite-horizon expression shown in the note:

$$
\pi_t
= \beta^{T+1}E_t\pi_{t+T+1}
  \kappa \sum_{k=0}^{T}\beta^k E_t\tilde{y}_{t+k}
$$

Math move: repeated substitution pushes the expectation term further into the future while accumulating output-gap terms.

Economic meaning: because expectations enter, inflation today depends on an entire expected *path* of future gaps, not only on today’s gap.

### Step 5 (Impose the transversality condition)

The lecture explicitly states the transversality condition:

$$
\lim_{T\to \infty}\beta^{T+1}E_t\pi_{t+T+1} = 0
$$

which gives the closed-form solution:

$$
\pi_t = \kappa \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\beta^k E_t\tilde{y}_{t+k}
$$

Math move: in the limit, the far-future term disappears, leaving a convergent infinite sum.

Economic meaning: in the baseline NKPC, inflation today is a discounted sum of expected future gaps. That is why expectations management is a first-order policy channel in this model.

## Derivation 2: Recursive Substitution For The DIS Equation

The same idea applies to the DIS equation: keep substituting the $t+1$ version into the $t$ version until the future term vanishes.
The course note shows the final result:

$$
\tilde{y}_t = -E_t\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\sigma}\left(r_{t+k}-r_{t+k}^n\right)
$$

Here is the step-by-step logic behind it.

### Step 0 (Starting point)

It is convenient to write DIS in real-rate form using the Fisher equation $r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$:

$$
\tilde{y}_t = E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(r_t - r_t^n)
$$

Math move: rewrite DIS as “today’s gap equals expected tomorrow’s gap minus the (scaled) real-rate gap.”

Economic meaning: demand is high when the real rate is below the natural real rate.

### Step 1 (Solve one period forward and take expectations)

At $t+1$:

$$
\tilde{y}_{t+1} = E_{t+1}\tilde{y}_{t+2} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(r_{t+1} - r_{t+1}^n)
$$

Taking $E_t[\cdot]$:

$$
E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} = E_t\tilde{y}_{t+2} - \frac{1}{\sigma}E_t(r_{t+1} - r_{t+1}^n)
$$

Math move: same expectations step as for the NKPC.

Economic meaning: households plan intertemporally, so today’s demand reacts to the expected path of real rates, not only today’s rate.

### Step 2 (Substitute back and iterate)

Substituting Step 1 into Step 0 pushes the expectation term forward and accumulates real-rate-gap terms.
After $T$ substitutions the note shows:

$$
\tilde{y}_t
= E_t\tilde{y}_{t+T+1}
-\frac{1}{\sigma}\sum_{k=0}^{T} E_t(r_{t+k}-r_{t+k}^n)
$$

### Step 3 (Transversality for the gap term)

Impose:

$$
\lim_{T\to \infty}E_t\tilde{y}_{t+T+1} = 0
$$

yielding:

$$
\tilde{y}_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} E_t(r_{t+k}-r_{t+k}^n)
$$

Economic meaning: the output gap is high when the real rate is expected to be below its natural benchmark for a sustained period.

## Derivation 3: From Three Equations To The 2x2 Matrix System

The slides move quickly from the 3-equation block to a compact 2x2 forward-looking system. This section fills in the missing algebra.

### Goal

We want a system in the two endogenous variables $(\tilde{y}_t,\pi_t)$ written in terms of expectations $(E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1},E_t\pi_{t+1})$
and the exogenous composite $(r_t^n - \rho - v_t)$.

### Step A: Substitute the Taylor rule into DIS (eliminate i_t)

Start with DIS:

$$
\tilde{y}_t = E_t \tilde{y}_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}\left(i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1} - r_t^n\right)
$$

Insert the interest-rate rule $i_t = \rho + \phi_\pi \pi_t + \phi_y \tilde{y}_t + v_t$:

$$
\tilde{y}_t
= E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}
- \frac{1}{\sigma}\left(\rho + \phi_\pi \pi_t + \phi_y \tilde{y}_t + v_t - E_t\pi_{t+1} - r_t^n\right)
$$

Multiply by $\sigma$ and collect the $\tilde{y}_t$ terms on the left:

$$
(\sigma+\phi_y)\tilde{y}_t
= \sigma E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} + E_t\pi_{t+1} + (r_t^n-\rho-v_t) - \phi_\pi \pi_t
$$

Math move: substitution + collecting terms.

Economic meaning: demand today depends on expected future demand, expected inflation, fundamentals (via $r_t^n$), and how the policy rule reacts to inflation (the $-\phi_\pi\pi_t$ term).

### Step B: Use the NKPC to eliminate the current π_t from the DIS line

From NKPC:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa\tilde{y}_t
$$

Substitute this into the right-hand side term $-\phi_\pi \pi_t$:

$$
(\sigma+\phi_y)\tilde{y}_t
= \sigma E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} + E_t\pi_{t+1} + (r_t^n-\rho-v_t) - \phi_\pi(\beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa\tilde{y}_t)
$$

Bring the $-\phi_\pi\kappa\tilde{y}_t$ term to the left:

$$
(\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa)\tilde{y}_t
= \sigma E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} + (1-\beta\phi_\pi)E_t\pi_{t+1} + (r_t^n-\rho-v_t)
$$

Divide by the common denominator:

$$
\tilde{y}_t
= \frac{\sigma}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}
 + \frac{1-\beta\phi_\pi}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}E_t\pi_{t+1}
 + \frac{1}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}(r_t^n-\rho-v_t)
$$

Math move: eliminate $\pi_t$ using NKPC, then solve for $\tilde{y}_t$.

Economic meaning: the denominator $\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa$ is “stabilization strength.” Stronger feedback makes it harder for expectations to create large movements today.

### Step C: Plug the solved y~_t back into NKPC to get the π_t line

Start again from NKPC:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \kappa\tilde{y}_t
$$

Insert the $\tilde{y}_t$ expression:

$$
\pi_t
= \frac{\sigma\kappa}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}
 + \left[\beta + \frac{\kappa(1-\beta\phi_\pi)}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}\right]E_t\pi_{t+1}
 + \frac{\kappa}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}(r_t^n-\rho-v_t)
$$

The slides simplify the square-bracket term to:

$$
\frac{\kappa + \beta(\sigma+\phi_y)}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}
$$

because:

$$
\beta(\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa) + \kappa(1-\beta\phi_\pi) = \beta(\sigma+\phi_y)+\kappa
$$

So the final inflation line is:

$$
\pi_t
= \frac{\sigma\kappa}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1}
 + \frac{\kappa + \beta(\sigma+\phi_y)}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}E_t\pi_{t+1}
 + \frac{\kappa}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}(r_t^n-\rho-v_t)
$$

### Step D: Write it in matrix form (exactly as in the slides)

Define:

$$
\Omega \equiv \frac{1}{\sigma+\phi_y+\phi_\pi\kappa}
$$

Then:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\tilde{y}_t \\
\pi_t
\end{bmatrix}
=
\Omega
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma & 1-\beta\phi_\pi \\
\sigma\kappa & \kappa + \beta(\sigma+\phi_y)
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} \\
E_t\pi_{t+1}
\end{bmatrix}
 + \Omega
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
\kappa
\end{bmatrix}
(r_t^n-\rho-v_t)
$$

### Uniqueness and the Taylor principle (as stated in the slides)

The lecture’s uniqueness claim is:

- Eigenvalue discipline: the absolute value of both eigenvalues of the forward-looking matrix must be below one (Blanchard-Kahn condition in the slides).

And the lecture provides the common sufficient condition (assuming $\phi_\pi,\phi_y \ge 0$):

$$
\kappa(\phi_\pi - 1) + (1-\beta)\phi_y > 0
$$

## Divine Coincidence (The Clean Baseline Benchmark)

The slide deck’s divine-coincidence statement is:

> If monetary policy perfectly stabilizes inflation, the output gap is also stabilized. This argument goes in both directions.

Here is the clean logic in the baseline NKPC without an explicit cost-push shock term.

### Direction 1: strict inflation targeting implies a zero output gap

If strict inflation targeting achieves $\pi_t = 0$ for all $t$, then in particular $E_t\pi_{t+1}=0$. Plug into NKPC:

$$
0 = \beta\cdot 0 + \kappa\tilde{y}_t \quad \Rightarrow \quad \tilde{y}_t = 0
$$

### Direction 2: a zero output gap implies inflation stability

If $\tilde{y}_t=0$ for all $t$, NKPC becomes:

$$
\pi_t = \beta E_t\pi_{t+1}
$$

Iterating forward and using the transversality condition gives $\pi_t=0$.
(This is exactly the same “recursive substitution + transversality” move you practiced above.)

### What the slides conclude from this

The deck states that a policy of strict inflation targeting achieves:

$$
\pi_t = 0,\qquad y_t = y_t^n
$$

and, using DIS, implies the “tracking” condition:

$$
i_t = r_t^n
$$

## Implementation: Why “i_t = r^n_t” Is Not Enough

Lecture 3 is explicit: the desired allocation is not the same as an implementable rule. The slide deck lists two problems with the rule:

$$
i_t = r_t^n
$$

### Practical problem: r^n_t is unobserved

$r_t^n$ is a model object, not a directly observed series.

### Technical problem: indeterminacy (sunspot equilibria)

The slides give a compact mechanism: if the interest rate does not respond to inflation or the output gap, expectations can drift and become self-fulfilling.
Here is the derivation the deck shows.

1. Start from the Fisher equation (slides):

   $$
   r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}
   $$

2. Under the rule $i_t = r_t^n$, the real-rate gap becomes:

   $$
   r_t - r_t^n = -E_t\pi_{t+1}
   $$

3. Plug into DIS:

   $$
   \tilde{y}_t = E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} - \frac{1}{\sigma}(r_t - r_t^n)
   $$

   yielding:

   $$
   \tilde{y}_t = E_t\tilde{y}_{t+1} + \frac{1}{\sigma}E_t\pi_{t+1}
   $$

4. Together with NKPC, you now have two forward-looking variables $(\tilde{y}_t,\pi_t)$, but policy is not providing the stabilizing feedback that selects a unique equilibrium.

### The lecture’s fix: add “threat” terms (Rule 2)

The slides propose:

$$
i_t = r_t^n + \phi_\pi \pi_t + \phi_y \tilde{y}_t
$$

The equilibrium outcome can still be $i_t = r_t^n$ in the desired equilibrium, but the extra terms matter because they make deviations expensive:
they anchor expectations by threatening a strong reaction if inflation/gap tries to drift away.

### A simpler practical approximation (Rule 3)

The slides then emphasize an observable-only rule:

$$
i_t = \rho + \phi_\pi\pi_t
$$

with a high $\phi_\pi$ as a way to approximate the optimal allocation while remaining implementable.

## Optimal Policy With Permanent Supply Shocks (What The Slides Emphasize)

Lecture 3 evaluates policy by comparing the real rate to the natural real rate $r_t^n$. The slides give the natural-rate expression:

$$
r_t^n = \rho - \sigma\frac{1+\phi}{\sigma+\phi}(1-\rho_a)a_t + (1-\rho_z)z_t
$$

If technology shocks are permanent, $\rho_a=1$, so $(1-\rho_a)=0$ and the technology term drops out.
That is why the lecture concludes: under strict inflation targeting, monetary policy does not need to respond to permanent supply shocks in this setup (because $r_t^n$ does not move from that source).

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls

- Mixing up natural output $y_t^n$ with “potential output” from a statistical filter. In this lecture it is a model concept.
- Saying “the model is forward-looking” without being able to show the recursive-substitution steps and the transversality condition.
- Thinking the Taylor principle is a slogan rather than a determinacy condition tied to eigenvalues/sunspots.
- Forgetting that divine coincidence relies on the baseline NKPC without an explicit cost-push wedge; later lectures change that equation.
- Conflating “optimal allocation” with an “implementable rule.” Lecture 3 spends multiple slides on why that gap matters.
